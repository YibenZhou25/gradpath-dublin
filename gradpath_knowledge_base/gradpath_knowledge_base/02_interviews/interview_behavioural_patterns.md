# Behavioural Interview Patterns for Graduate / Junior Roles

> Practical guidance on answering behavioural ("tell me about a time...") questions
> at Dublin tech employers, with attention to common pitfalls for international
> students whose educational background did not include extensive 1-on-1
> behavioural-style assessments.

## The STAR framework — what it actually means

STAR is the standard structure expected for behavioural answers:

- **Situation** — the context: where, when, who was involved
- **Task** — what specifically you needed to do or solve
- **Action** — what YOU (not your team) did, step by step
- **Result** — measurable outcome, plus what you learned

A common mistake by graduates is to spend 70% of the answer on Situation and Task,
then rush Action and Result. **The right ratio is roughly 10/10/60/20**: minimal
context, brief task framing, detailed actions, and a substantive result.

## The 8 questions that cover 80% of behavioural rounds

Most behavioural interviews at Dublin tech employers can be predicted by preparing
strong answers to these eight questions:

### Q1. "Tell me about yourself"

- Not strictly behavioural, but always asked first
- Target: 60-90 seconds, 3-part structure: who you are now → relevant background → why you want this role
- Common failure: chronological recital of CV. Better: thematic narrative

### Q2. "Tell me about a time you faced a technical challenge"

- They want to see how you debug, when you ask for help, how you persist
- Strong answers include: a specific bug or design problem, the dead-ends you hit,
  what eventually worked, what you learned about your own thinking

### Q3. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a teammate"

- DO NOT answer "I never disagree, I'm very collaborative"
- They are looking for: ability to advocate for a position, listen to the other side,
  reach resolution without ego
- Source material: group projects, code reviews, design choices on internships

### Q4. "Tell me about a time you led something"

- Leadership ≠ being a manager. Acceptable examples: leading a coursework team,
  organising a hackathon team, mentoring a junior peer, driving a project decision
- What they want: clear accountability, decisions you made, how you brought
  others along

### Q5. "Tell me about a time you failed"

- DO NOT answer with humble-brag ("I work too hard")
- Strong answers: a real misjudgement, what you tried that didn't work, how you
  recovered, the lasting change in how you work
- Project failures, a missed deadline, a poor design choice are all good material

### Q6. "Tell me about a time you took initiative"

- They want to see you doing more than the assigned task
- Examples: spotted a bug nobody else noticed, suggested a tool/process change,
  picked up an unowned area in a group project

### Q7. "Why this company / why this role?"

- Generic "you're a leader in tech" answers are obvious filler
- Strong answers reference: a specific product or engineering blog post, the team's
  problem domain, a value the company has stated that you connect with personally

### Q8. "Where do you see yourself in 3-5 years?"

- DO NOT answer "in your VP chair, ha ha"
- Strong answers: technical depth in a domain you find interesting, becoming the
  person juniors come to with questions, leading a small project — concrete,
  ambitious-but-realistic

## Common pitfalls for international students

Several patterns that hurt non-native-English candidates in behavioural rounds:

### Pitfall 1: Plural pronouns swallow individual contribution

Saying "we did X, we improved Y, we learned Z" makes interviewers unable to
score YOU. Force yourself to say "I did X" when describing actions. The team
context is fine, but YOUR specific actions need to be visible.

### Pitfall 2: Cultural modesty undersells achievements

In some cultures, claiming credit feels rude. In Western interviews, **not** claiming
credit for something you did is read as you not having done it. State your
contributions clearly. "I designed the database schema" is not bragging — it's
information the interviewer needs.

### Pitfall 3: Excessive context before the action

Interviews are time-bounded. Long context wastes the time you need for action and
result. Practice cutting situation/task to under 90 seconds combined.

### Pitfall 4: Treating "weakness" questions as a trap

When asked "what's your biggest weakness", give a real one with active mitigation.
Cliché non-answers ("I'm too detail-oriented") signal that you are unable or
unwilling to introspect.

### Pitfall 5: Translating idioms literally

If you mean "I took ownership", say "I took ownership", not "I took the
responsibility on my own back". Idiom-translation issues read as low fluency
even when your underlying English is strong.

## How to prepare a STAR answer bank

Before interviews, prepare 6-8 stories from your past 2 years. For each story,
tag which behavioural questions it can answer. A single strong story can often
answer 3-4 different questions with re-framing:

- A challenging group project → Q2 (technical challenge), Q3 (disagreement), Q4
  (leadership), Q6 (initiative)
- An internship task that went wrong → Q5 (failure), Q2 (challenge)
- A side project / open-source contribution → Q6 (initiative), Q4 (leadership), Q2

For each story, write down the STAR structure on paper. Practice telling each
in 3 minutes. **Out-loud practice is essential** — written preparation alone does
not transfer well to live interview performance.

## What "good" sounds like

A well-structured behavioural answer typically:
- Names a specific timeframe ("during my master's project last semester...")
- Names specific people / roles where relevant ("our team of 4")
- Quantifies where possible ("reduced runtime from 30s to under 5s")
- States what you learned, even if implicitly ("I now check for X before
  starting Y")
- Closes cleanly without trailing off
