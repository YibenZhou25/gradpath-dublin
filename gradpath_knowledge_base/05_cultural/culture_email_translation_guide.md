# Chinese-Style → Irish-Style Workplace Email Translation Guide

> Side-by-side examples of common email-writing situations, contrasting Chinese
> conventions with Irish workplace conventions, with explanations of why each
> shift matters. Practical reference for international students entering the
> Irish workplace.

## Why translation matters more than language

Most Chinese international students reach Ireland with sufficient written
English to communicate. The gap is not vocabulary — it's **rhetorical
convention**. Irish workplace emails follow patterns that, if missed, can:
- Make a request sound more demanding than intended
- Make an apology sound either insincere or excessive
- Make a refusal sound rude when politeness was intended
- Make a thank-you sound flat when warmth was intended

Each section below shows a typical situation in two versions. The first is the
direct translation of a Chinese-convention email. The second is the
Irish-workplace equivalent.

## Pattern 1 · Asking a colleague for help

### Chinese-translation version

> Subject: Need help
>
> Hi John,
>
> Sorry to bother you. I have a question about the deployment script.
> Could you help me?
>
> Thanks,
> Wei

### Irish-workplace version

> Subject: Quick question on the deployment script
>
> Hi John,
>
> Hope you're doing well. I'm trying to figure out the new deployment
> script — specifically the part where we set environment variables. I've
> read through the README but I'm not sure if I should be using the staging
> config or the prod one for the dev environment.
>
> Would you have 10 minutes sometime this week to walk me through it?
> No rush at all — happy to grab a coffee whenever suits you.
>
> Thanks,
> Wei

### Why the difference matters

- **Subject line**: "Need help" forces the recipient to open the email to
  understand. "Quick question on the deployment script" tells them what's
  coming and signals it's not urgent.
- **Opening**: "Hope you're doing well" is expected social glue, not optional
  formality. Skipping it reads as transactional.
- **Specifics first**: showing what you've already tried ("I've read through
  the README") signals you've done the work. Asking blind reads as lazy.
- **Sizing the ask**: "10 minutes" makes the ask feel small. "Help me" is
  open-ended and harder to commit to.
- **Releasing pressure**: "No rush at all" lets the recipient prioritise
  appropriately. Without it, every email reads as urgent.
- **Apologising less**: "Sorry to bother you" is unnecessary in normal
  workplace exchanges. Apologise when something has actually gone wrong, not
  for asking a normal work question.

## Pattern 2 · Pushing back on a deadline

### Chinese-translation version

> Hi Sarah,
>
> I am very sorry but I cannot finish the report by Friday. I need more
> time. Sorry for the inconvenience.
>
> Best,
> Wei

### Irish-workplace version

> Hi Sarah,
>
> Quick update on the quarterly report — I want to flag that the data-
> analysis piece is taking longer than I'd planned. The customer-event
> dataset has more edge cases than I anticipated and I want to handle them
> properly rather than rush.
>
> Realistically, I can have a solid draft to you by Tuesday next week. If
> you need something earlier for the all-hands, I can put together a
> partial version (sections 1-3) by Friday as planned, and complete the
> rest by Tuesday.
>
> Let me know which works better — happy to adjust.
>
> Thanks,
> Wei

### Why the difference matters

- **No upfront apology**: leading with "I am very sorry" puts the recipient on
  the defensive and makes the email about your failure. Lead with the
  business situation.
- **Show why**: "data has more edge cases" makes the delay legitimate.
  Without context it sounds like missed planning.
- **Offer options**: a single "I can't" is harder to receive than "here are
  two options". Always give the manager choice.
- **Frame as professional judgement**: "I want to handle them properly rather
  than rush" frames the delay as quality, not failure.

## Pattern 3 · Saying you don't know something

### Chinese-translation version

> Hi team,
>
> I am not sure about this. I will research and reply later.

### Irish-workplace version

> Good question — I don't have a confident answer off the top of my head.
> Let me check the latest dashboard data and the engineering wiki, and I'll
> come back to you by end of day with a proper answer.

### Why the difference matters

- **"I don't know" is fine**: in Irish workplaces, saying you don't know
  is professional. Pretending to know and being wrong is worse.
- **Naming what you'll do**: "I'll check the dashboard and the wiki" shows
  initiative and gives the questioner an estimate.
- **Time-bound commitment**: "by end of day" beats "later" — gives the
  questioner a clear expectation.

## Pattern 4 · Declining a meeting

### Chinese-translation version

> Sorry, I cannot attend the meeting tomorrow. Sorry for any inconvenience.

### Irish-workplace version

> Thanks for the invite — I won't be able to join tomorrow as I'm in the
> design review at the same time. Could you share the meeting notes
> afterwards? Happy to provide async input on anything specific you'd like
> my read on.

### Why the difference matters

- **Thank for the invite**: small but expected.
- **Reason without over-apologising**: state the conflict simply.
- **Stay engaged**: offering to read notes and provide async input shows you
  care about the topic, not just escaping the meeting.

## Pattern 5 · Following up on something that's been ignored

### Chinese-translation version

> Hi Tom,
>
> Just checking — did you receive my previous email about the API spec?
> I sent it two weeks ago. Please let me know.

### Irish-workplace version

> Hi Tom,
>
> Hope your week's going well. Just gently bumping this — I sent over the
> API spec a couple of weeks back and wanted to check whether you'd had a
> chance to look at it, or if you'd prefer I package it differently for
> easier review.
>
> Happy to walk through it on a quick call if that's easier.

### Why the difference matters

- **Soft language**: "gently bumping" signals you know they're busy and aren't
  blaming them.
- **Offer alternatives**: "package it differently" or "walk through it" gives
  the recipient ways to engage if the original format wasn't ideal.
- **Avoid blame language**: "did you receive my previous email" can be read as
  accusing them of ignoring you, even when not intended.

## Pattern 6 · Asking your manager about a promotion or raise

### Chinese-translation version

> Hi Manager,
>
> I have been working hard for one year. I would like to discuss a
> promotion. Please let me know your thoughts.

### Irish-workplace version

> Hi Manager,
>
> I'd love to set up some time to talk about my growth and trajectory here.
> Looking at where I was when I started and where I am now — I've taken on
> the X project, started leading Y, and feel I've gone deeper on Z than the
> grad bar.
>
> I'd like to discuss what the path to senior engineer (or whatever the next
> level is) looks like, what the main gaps would be from your perspective, and
> what timeline might be realistic.
>
> Could we book 30 minutes for this in the next couple of weeks?

### Why the difference matters

- **Frame around growth, not just title/money**: starting with "promotion"
  feels transactional. Starting with "growth and trajectory" opens a
  collaborative conversation.
- **Bring evidence**: name specific things you've done that signal readiness.
- **Ask about the path, not the destination**: "what would the gaps be" shows
  willingness to develop, not entitlement.
- **Concrete logistical ask**: a 30-minute booked conversation is easier to
  commit to than "discuss".

## General principles to internalise

After studying the patterns above, the core moves are:

1. **Lead with context, not apology** — open with what the situation is, not
   with sorry-for-bothering-you
2. **Show your work** — what have you already tried? what do you know?
3. **Offer options** — make it easy to say yes or to redirect
4. **Be specific** — numbers, dates, sections, not vague "later" or "soon"
5. **Use social glue** — "hope you're well" is not optional in Ireland
6. **Match the register of the recipient** — copy the style they use back to
   them

These are not aesthetic preferences. They are mechanics that make Irish
workplace emails work.
